Internal Council

Work project · Multi-voice reflection to surface perspectives and make better decisions.

Overview

The Internal Council is a reflective tool: it simulates distinct “inner voices” (e.g., Inner Child, Empath, Self-critic, Warrior, Executor, Sage, and Body) to explore situations from multiple angles. By hearing each perspective in its own voice—without diluting or moderating—the user can separate emotions, critique, advocacy, planning, wisdom, and bodily signals into clear threads before choosing what to do next.

How it works

  1. Describe the situation. The user provides a short narrative of what’s going on.
  2. Council goal-setting. The tool states what the exercise aims to help with (clarity, options, tradeoffs).
  3. One voice at a time. Each Council member responds in its own style—raw and unfiltered.
  4. Resonance check. The tool asks which responses resonate (or don’t) to refine direction.
  5. Next steps. Council collaborates to outline actions, scripts, boundaries, or experiments.
  6. Optional lenses. Invite other viewpoints (e.g., Stoic, coach, public-health lens) if helpful.

Council members (examples)

  • Inner Child — “I feel …” Raw emotions, needs, play, and creativity.
  • Empath — “I can understand …” Interprets others’ motives and feelings.
  • Self-critic — Quality control; surfaces hard truths and plausible external critiques.
  • Warrior — Boundaries, self-respect, safety, and courage under pressure.
  • Executor — Planning, tradeoffs, feasibility, and decision criteria.
  • Sage — Big-picture patterns, long-term consequences, and values.
  • Body — Somatic signals, regulation, and self-care prompts.

When to use it

  • Conflicted decisions (career, relationships, creative direction).
  • Debriefing emotionally charged meetings or feedback.
  • Boundary-setting and difficult conversations (prepare scripts).
  • Post-mortems: separate signals (facts vs. feelings vs. fears).

How to use (typical flow)

  1. Paste a short scenario or write 5–8 sentences of context.
  2. Skim each member’s first pass; mark what resonates and what doesn’t.
  3. Ask follow-ups (e.g., “What would be a respectful boundary here?”).
  4. Collect concrete next steps (e.g., a one-paragraph plan or a short script).
